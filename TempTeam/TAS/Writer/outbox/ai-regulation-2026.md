# AI Regulation in Early 2026: What Is in Effect and Where Each Major Jurisdiction Stands

The past eighteen months have produced binding regulatory obligations in some jurisdictions and deliberate deregulation in others. For companies building AI products that cross borders, the result is a compliance landscape that varies significantly depending on which market a product serves.

## European Union: First Obligations Now Enforceable

The EU AI Act has been in force since August 2024, but its rules take effect in phases. The first binding obligations, covering prohibited AI practices and AI literacy requirements, entered into application on February 2, 2025, according to the official EU AI Act implementation timeline. Rules for general-purpose AI model providers followed on August 2, 2025.

The next major deadline is August 2, 2026, when rules for high-risk AI systems and full enforcement powers at national and EU level take effect. Penalties for violations of the prohibited practices provisions can reach 35 million euros or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher, as detailed by LegalNodes in its 2026 compliance analysis.

The Act applies to any business placing AI systems on the EU market or affecting EU residents, regardless of where that business is headquartered. Risk classification, technical documentation, and in some cases conformity assessments are required before deployment for high-risk applications.

## United States: Deregulation Federally, Fragmentation at State Level

On January 23, 2025, President Trump signed an executive order revoking the Biden administration's 2023 AI safety order and directing federal agencies to remove barriers to AI development. In December 2025, a second executive order instructed the Justice Department to establish a task force to challenge state AI laws the administration considers inconsistent with federal policy.

In March 2026, the White House released a National Policy Framework for AI as legislative recommendations to Congress. The framework takes what the administration describes as a "light touch" approach, emphasizing innovation and federal preemption of state AI rules.

Those state rules are already in force. California, Colorado, New York, and several other states enacted AI-specific laws in 2025 covering transparency disclosures, automated decision-making obligations, and high-risk system requirements. Until Congress passes a federal statute, companies operating across state lines must navigate overlapping requirements. Wilson Sonsini's 2026 regulatory preview identifies this fragmentation as the primary near-term compliance risk for US-market AI development.

## China: Targeted Rules Rather Than a Single Framework

China has not pursued a comprehensive AI statute. Instead, it has enacted separate regulations by use case: rules for recommendation algorithms, synthetic media, and generative AI are each in force under distinct instruments.

In October 2025, China's legislature passed amendments to the Cybersecurity Law that added AI provisions, bringing AI into national law for the first time. Those amendments took effect on January 1, 2026, according to IAPP's Asia-Pacific regulatory tracker. The State Council's AI Plus Action Plan, issued in August 2025, outlines national AI infrastructure investment priorities rather than additional restrictions.

## United Kingdom: No Comprehensive Law Yet

The UK entered 2026 without dedicated AI legislation. Its current framework relies on existing sector regulators applying cross-cutting principles to AI, as set out in the government's 2023 pro-innovation white paper. In February 2025, the government rebranded the AI Safety Institute as the AI Security Institute, indicating a shift in focus toward misuse and national security risks.

A dedicated AI Bill has been discussed as a potential 2026 legislative priority, according to White & Case's UK regulatory tracker, but had not been introduced as of early 2026. The UK remains the least prescriptive of the four major jurisdictions for now.

## What This Means in Practice

The EU's extraterritorial scope is the most immediate operational pressure for global product teams. Any AI product serving EU users is in scope regardless of where its developer is based, and the August 2026 high-risk enforcement deadline is approaching.

In the United States, the practical challenge is the inverse: too many jurisdictions rather than too few. Mayer Brown's March 2026 analysis of the White House framework notes that until federal preemption is established by statute, state-level requirements will continue to diverge. Companies deploying AI in multiple US states are managing compliance programs that look more like international compliance than domestic.
